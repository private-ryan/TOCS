So you’ve decided to start your own business…What next?

By rebecca / 09 Apr 2013 / No Comments

So you’ve decided to start your own business…You want the benefits of running your own time; flexibility of family life balance; work space; environment and to pursue a career around your interests. Great! However, what if you feel uncertain about how to proceed – in other words – how to get clients? As a new freelancer or business owner, you’re sure to be an eager beaver and get the concept of your business into a money making enterprise!  What are the pros and cons of starting out that face you as a newbie?

First things first: Have you done your research? What are your rates? Will you charge for extra work? How do you want to get paid – hourly, on a day rate or per project? How will you agree to projects or set up appointments? Why not find out what the market average is in your area and charge a similar amount. If you’re going for high end work – do you charge clients more than the ‘going rate’?  Here is a great blog post about  how to price your services. It has a comprehensive list of what to take into account. 

Know who you’re working for: As a fail-safe shouldn’t you have an idea of who it is you’re going to be working with for the next chapter in your life? Make sure you know the basics about your new customer. What is your client’s name? Registered business name? Billing address? Contact numbers? Email address? Make sure you share the same information about yourself in your contract as well; so the client knows where and when they can contact you. Why not mention what you don’t do – as well as what you do, do. That way you’re upfront from the beginning and not caught in the trap of doing work you don’t really want to be doing to stay afloat.

Contacting clients: How do you intend to communicate with clients? Email, postal docs, phone? Be aware that although email is the standard way of communicating (and can often be perceived as being informal), anything you say or agree to could be seen as being ‘set in stone’. This is a great article about email and the legal connotations to your written word!

Do you want to get paid at the end of the day? Do you let people tell you that you have to work for free or assign yourself to a low-paying job -  if you want to crack into your chosen industry?  If you don’t take the job, there well may be a hundred more freelancers behind you willing to take that position, so why not let them and hold out for something that you feel deserves your time and expertise?

To do or not to do: Once you’ve started up, many friends and family will cotton on on the fact you have these skills and may suggest you ‘do them a favour’. No one can blame you for wanting to lend a hand to your family or ‘a friend of a friend’. But ask yourself this…Will you have a good word of mouth recommendation after the work is complete? Or will helping out a mate actually burn a friendship and end up with a job you’re not too happy with? Before you embark on this ‘informal’ work, why not just put a few things in place to cover your back – and your client’s. That way you both know what to expect from one another. Some freelancers have told us how they’ve been burned by doing a favour for a friend, where sadly communications were misunderstood and they’ve not come out of the situation favourably through no fault of their own, other than that they didn’t treat it as a job. Have a look at these 5 Free to Use Freelance Design Contract Templates to figure out what suits you.

Why not start as you mean to go on: Be as professional as you can be. Make sure you understand and research common practices in your chosen freelancing industry. Check out these quick start guides here.

Get paid for the time you put in: Should you include a retainer fee in a contract?  Don’t under estimate hours of concept time that you’ll put into a project at the beginning. An up front fee ensures that you are actually paid for all the work that you’ve done up to the point the client notifies you that they’re not going to continue on with the project. This could be down to any number of things – but you don’t want to end up forking out on the project only to have the rug pulled out from underneath you, with no job or money in the bank! Just because you are a fledgling in a world of established freelancers – doesn’t mean you shouldn’t command respect from the get-go!

Have you thought about insurance? Could the advice you give or work you do cause someone a mischief if interpreted the wrong way? It is always best to cover yourself – so your loved ones or dog won’t go hungry. Check out this link to see an overview of the various types of insurance you may need as a Director, employer or freelancer. Check out this list for a breakdown of all things you may need to insure against as a small business owner!

Don’t forget the paperwork: HMRC related paperwork can build up before you you know it – especially if you’re an employer. Make sure you familiarise yourself with all that is required of you. Living the dream means being super responsible for it too. Here is a guide to the new ‘real time’ PAYE basics.  These tips are also useful for knowing what you need to think about when running your freelancing career.
