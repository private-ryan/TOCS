Can your employees care about your business as much as you do?

By rebecca / 22 Jan 2013 / No Comments

As a small business you may have started out as a one man band operation and put in all the hours of hard graft to get the business doing well enough to grow and need staff to run your bigger empire. But how do you guarantee productivity and a workforce that care about the business as much as you do, in this new role of becoming an employer?

Trust your instincts: Obviously you have to hire the right person; someone you feel will commit to the role and shows determination and passion in what they’re doing. As a small business owner, you also need to make sure the chemistry for working closely together is right. You can teach the right person how you want things to be done – you need to make sure you can work well together more than anything else. At Forward space we share desk space and so need to know that working in such close quarters with each-other means we can stand to be literally in each others face for 8 hours a day!

Don’t ask someone to do something you wouldn’t be prepared to do yourself: This doesn’t set your employees a good example and shows you have double standards. Do the job you want your employees to do! Be the example you want in your workplace, your employees will take note of how to conduct themselves and fit in with the tone of your organisation.

Don’t ‘Dangle the Carrot’: How is that anyway to treat anybody? If you want a work force that respects you and works as hard as they can to get results or strive to continue on your success, then show them respect. If you’ve hired the right person in the first place – let them get on with their job. Standing over someone’s shoulders and telling them that they’re doing wrong is no way to instill confidence and initiative. Why not let employees learn from their own mistakes? Guaranteed they won’t do it again and you can support them through why whatever it was they did – didn’t work out.

Keep it flat: Flat organisiations work really well because employees and employers can decide and collaborate on projects together. This gives your staff more power in how your company is run, and in-turn will make employees care about what they’re doing more – as they’ve made a decision in how/where to take the company forward. Listen to employees when they approach you with ideas or concerns. Acknowledge their feelings and show them that you value their input and experiences. This will ensure that any problems are dealt with openly and you can all trust each other to look out for one another!

Empower learning: Why not ask your staff what they want to learn or what they think will make them do their job better? By bringing their interests into the mix, they’ll work even harder at the job in hand and appreciate you are interested in what they enjoy doing. This also gives them the confidence to reflect on their own strengths and weaknesses and consider what they need to work on to improve any skill gaps they might have.

Reward the extra mile: If you feel your staff are working hard and putting in the time to get things and going above and beyond the call of duty then aknowledge their hard work and commitment to your company. You don’t have to pay huge bonuses and do crazy gestures of appreciation. Treating someone to a day out or lunch as a thank you can be enough to make someone feel valued and trusted in the job they do. As a small business you can’t afford a high turnover of staff, and so building a strong team is the most cost effective way of ensuring longevity from your staff and consistency for your customers.

Always check in: Always ask how an employee’s day was and what they did that day – this way you can find out any issues and make sure they’re on track with their work. If they’re struggling with something find out why, do you have too many expectations of someone? or do they have things on their mind they just cant shift. We’re not saying that being lenient or a million miles away from what your employees get up to is the way to nurture staff, just trust the person you’ve hired to do their job well. They’ll want to prove to you they can full-fill your beliefs and achieve shared goals for the business. Giving someone an inch doesn’t mean they’ll always take a mile.
