Is Co-working all Smoke and Mirrors?

29 Nov 2012 / No Comments

How do you encompass the very best ways of working? Incorporating the flexibility of working from home and relaxed atmosphere of a café? Add to the mix a professional interior, collaboration and a vibrant business community where freelancers and independent workers get do their 9-5 (or not, in most cases!) and you’ve got yourself a co-working space! But what else does co-working really mean, and is it really…real? Or is it all smoke and mirrors?

One advantage of living near a co-working space is having access to a registered business address on your doorstep. This is of course, the ultimate smoke and mirrors effect that co-working offers to the small business owner. Giving the impression you work in an office, yet you actually work from home.  This allows you to receive any correspondence from clients without the need to hand out your personal details; you can publish and give out your undercover office address to clients and any other correspondence you have – as your own headquarters!

Another great illusion of a co-working space, are the meeting rooms. Instead of meeting clients and associates at a coffee shop or the home office, client meetings can take place in a more formal environment. You can also use conference rooms to pitch an idea to a potential client and hold events of your own – that in your home office you might not find the space for.

Can your illusion ever be foiled? Most co-working spaces have ground rules that limit banter, obviously with other people around there is going to be background noise; possibly giving away the fact you don’t work in private offices. If you are on the phone a lot and need a super silent environment to work in, then shared space may not meet your needs. However when you’re checking out a potential co-working space, ask if there are ‘quiet zones’ or conference rooms you can book for an impromptu meeting or call, any host will be happy to meet your needs and be as flexible as you need them to be. But hark, according to a study http://www.deskmag.com/en/ambient-noise-boosts-creativity-coworking-spaces-493 on the effects of noise and creativity, ambient noise is not necessarily a bad thing. It was seen to improve creative cognition!

The great thing about a co-working space is that you can be all things to all people, if you so choose to be. You can meet with local clients at your nearest and dearest space one day and the next hop on a train to London and meet and greet another client there. Shared space means that you don’t have a formal lease contract to tie you in for months at a time and you can end your use of the space when it suits you! Another illusion that co-working brings to the table, you can have your own office anywhere in the country, and around the world!

The biggest advantage of working in a shared space, is that you can join forces with other entrepreneurs when you lack certain skills you need for a job – ensuring you don’t lose out on work. The collaborative culture within a co-working space is no accident; once people hear what you can do, or have enjoyed working with you and your client/s then things can only grow and develop for your business. Having a community of partnerships can make your business seem more than a one man band, and for some of your clients, that can seem an attractive proposition!

 Is co-working really…real? For many freelancers it’s a way of life, but for others it is a very temporary space and not necessarily an ideal set-up. The nature of a shared space with so many freelance businesses is that it can be a very transient place; so you won’t necessarily know everyone all of the time or stay in touch with others you’ve worked with. To some, the space is a great way to grow a business; with the smoke and mirrors effect it offers, then moving onto a bigger space once they’ve out grown what a co-working space can provide.
