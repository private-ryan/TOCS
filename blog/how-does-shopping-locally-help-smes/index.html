How does shopping locally help SMEs?

By rebecca / 13 Dec 2012 / No Comments

1. Towns and Cities that preserve their independent businesses give the local culture a unique character and distinctive shopping experience to consumers over other generic high street locations. This means the community stands out from the crowd and helps to drive up interest in other economic factors such as tourism and attracting new talent to settle in the town – further contributing to the culture of the place, giving it more longevity.

2. If a town has more independent shops and businesses, then the impact of decisions are felt more greatly. More involvement from a town on a ground level means that residents and business owners can have a say in how the culture of where they live is shaped – rather than having generic businesses and chains telling them what to buy.

3.Since the economic downturn the government is recognising that entrepreneurship is an asset and emerging fuel to the economy. Innovation and prosperity through independent businesses means new job opportunities for people in the small town; giving the unemployed a job or low paid workers the ability to move out of their low socioeconomic status and gain more satisfaction in their day to day role.

5. A marketplace of many small businesses is the best way to ensure diversity, creativity and competitive prices. Smaller businesses tend to put the needs and interests of  local consumers first, meaning a more bespoke service – guaranteeing a much broader range of choices and relevance to the customer. Our freedom of choice can be hindered by larger ‘big box’ chains. Despite having an array of ‘economy’ to the very ‘finest’ of produce, corporate chains cannot always guarantee the customer centred service as much as a smaller retailer is able to. For example due to national sales targets, a new product you like may be discontinued due to various policies in store – a small retailer has far more freedom when it comes to buying in niche produce.

6.By supporting your local shop, you are financially helping other businesses on the high street. Local businesses tend to buy local produce or services themselves; requiring less transportation of goods and also encouraging other businesses to the town centre; rather than setting up shop on the outskirts of town. This means that more of your money stays within the community. Here is a great article about how shopping locally can boost the economy as a whole.

7.Word of mouth is the best PR and marketing tool for a small business; as consumers trust friends and acquaintances much more than they do an ad campaign or deal offer, meaning you get a good genuine bargain!

8.Community-based businesses are essential to local charities as business owners frequently serve on local boards as trustees and actively support causes - as many SMEs affiliate themselves with charities as part of a marketing strategy.

9. It’s not just trade or product based businesses that are cottoning on to this new trend of reverting back to your friendly neighborhood convenience; the tech industry is creating apps that can help consumers find local stores or shops; from restaurants, florists, taxi companies to coffee shops .  Larger national companies are too tapping into being ‘your local’ shop, concentrating  their efforts to develop smaller outlets, such as ‘express’ shops and for example  ‘Sainsbury’s Local’ stores. This doesn’t necessarily help independent shops, but it does demonstrate the power of a local mindset!

10.Many people are now changing the way in-which they do their weekly shop. Getting the majority from local shops and topping up with a supermarket splurge. Conscientious consumers are now interested in where their meat or produce has come from and what impact their grocery shopping has on the environment, local businesses and economy at large.

We are by no means saying that all small businesses are pure perfection, and that all corporate chains are all by default bad but the accumulative impacts of a town in touch with local culture and decision making is clear: local businesses do play a vital role in any community and by keeping the high street at the forefront of your budget, you encourage community interests. The life quality of a community changes when businesses and priorities move away from the heart of a town, our community is our home – so why not invest in it?
