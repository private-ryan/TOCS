Don’t lose out on claiming expenses

By rebecca / 12 Oct 2012 / 2 Comments

As a small business there is a lot of information to digest about what financial obligations you have. If you’re starting up, what should you think about in the way of finances? How do you make sure you don’t lose out?

1. Choose a legal status and structure for your business: Register with HMRC, and declare that you are self employed,  and what kind of business you are running. Are you Sole Trader, Limited Company, a Partnership, Limited Liability Partisanship, or even a Charity? Barbara Bacon gave us this tip on her workshop: Make sure that you register your business with HMRC within 3 months of starting to trade, otherwise you may get a fine.

2. Make sure you know which bracket of NI you have to pay, you can get loads of great straight talking info just here

3. Record keeping may seem like a boring chore, but just remember that if you know what’s going on with your financial situation, you know how your business is doing all round. This can help you decide where you need to take your business, which services you provide are most successful and which others need a bit of TLC. If you know record keeping just ain’t your thang – then invest in a good local bookkeeper or accountant that will make life easier for you. If you want to do things yourself, then there is plenty of free or low cost software options out there for you to try, which generally give you a month trial before you decide to officially sign up.

4. Did you know that you can claim back pre-trading expenses? For example any IT equipment that you bought before trading, that you now use for your business. Be sure to seek advice by a professional, to make sure that anything you claim back as a pre-trading expense is legit.

5. If you work from home you can deduct a percentage of mortgage or rent payments, insurance, household utilities and maintenance.

6. Do you know what you can or cannot claim for? Here is a comprehensive article about what you can claim back, if you’re running a Ltd company. Here is some no-nonsense advice for Sole Trader claims.

7. If your business has grown from strength to strength and you’re now looking to employ staff, what should you do? Taking the first steps can be tricky, but have no fear, get all your info here .Business Link also offers a huge amount of valuable information for this subject. If you’ve gone through the employment process and you’ve hired someone, make sure you register your shiny new employee with HMRC by declaring you are now a real life Employer! You will need to give HMRC a lot of info, but you can find this all out here

8. Do you know the minimum rate to pay an employee?

  • £6.19 - If your employee is 21 and over
  • £4.98 -  If your employee is 18-20 years of age
  • £3.68 - If your employee is 16-17 years of age
  • £2.65 – If you have hired an apprentices under 19 years of age.  (Correct October 2012).

9. What counts as a non-taxable expense? If you are self-employed you’ll have a wide range of running costs and expenses to think about. First things first; you can’t deduct any ‘private expenses’ i.e items for your own personal use. However if you use an item for both business and non-business use, you can reclaim VAT for the proportion of the item that you use for your business. For example if you use your car for both business and pleasure you’ll only be able to reclaim the VAT on the proportion that relates to ‘business’ use. 10. Did you know depending on your business, you may entitled to tax relief or allowance? For example: If you’re a farmer, market gardener or artist you might be able to reduce your tax bill by averaging out  your profits over two years. Other circumstances can also entitle you to a bit of tax relief, such as if if your business has made a loss. Be sure to seek advice on how to organise your finances if this applies to you.
