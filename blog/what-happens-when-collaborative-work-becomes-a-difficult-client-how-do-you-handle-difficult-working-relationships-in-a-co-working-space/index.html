What happens when a collaborative partner becomes a difficult client? How do you handle a difficult working relationship in a co-working space?

By rebecca / 06 Aug 2012 / No Comments

Communication is the key

1. First of all, try not to take things too personally: Has the way your new project panned out, differed slightly from how you thought it might be? Try to detach emotionally from the situation, and concentrate on the job at hand. You don’t want to let one project taint others that might work better for you!

2. Don’t let your wires get crossed: If things have become a bit of a mishmash in communication, and you’re just not on the same page, talk to your collaborative partner in person or over the phone (never through email!), to get things on the straight and narrow, as  things can easily be taken out of context.  If you feel things have been added to the project, that you weren’t quite expecting, go back to square one and outline the original ideas and agreement that initially brought you together. Don’t let additional work get out of hand and be clear what it is you all want to achieve out of this collaboration. If emails have been sent back and forth, remember to keep them for future reference.  If you feel the situation is that you just can’t talk to each other, then an email can be used as a tactful communication, if done right.

3. Bring it back to the beginning: As people who run your own businesses’, you’re likely to have strong opinions and beliefs. Sometimes they’re right, but on occasion they might be wrong! If you find you’re being rubbed up the wrong way, be sure to be diplomatic with how you approach things. A good way of doing this is to ask questions – rather than to make a public service announcement about feeling cheesed off! If you feel that the communication has gone slightly adrift, then bring it back. As hard as it is, address any issues as soon as possible – don’t let things hang in the air for too long.

Be diplomatic about how you approach things

4. Don’t let negative talk give you self-doubt: if your partner is pulling apart your greatest idea, separate yourself from it and look at it objectively. Criticisms are not easy to take, but when working together you have to take the rough with the smooth!

5. Be assertive rather than being just plain scary:Just because you share workspace with someone, doesn’t mean you have to change how you do things, but you are now working together. Don’t feel you can’t say what you really think, just make sure you’re sensitive about it and definitely don’t throw your toys out of the pram! Simply stick to the facts and be sure to explain and justify your point of view. Above all, remaining professional. Don’t get too hot under the collar and you won’t go wrong!

6. It’s always nice, to be nice: Even if the work with your collaborative partner was difficult, just remember that from this experience you’ve learnt a new skill set or at the very least they’ve given you new insight. Just because it may have not been an easy path, doesn’t mean that nothing has been gained, let them know what you’ve learnt from the experience. Maybe things didn’t pan out how you thought they would, because they too felt it was not what they’d expected.

Be honest about what you can and can’t deliver    

7. You can’t provide the promise:  You agreed to move the earth and now you just can’t deliver!  From the start it is best to be honest when collaborating with a fellow co-worker. It’s best to set a timeline that you’re comfortable with from the beginning. If you see the project is getting harder to achieve in the time you have, then it is time to face the music! Things come up all the time that move deadlines back and forth, and if there is a sound reason that you need to change things; your partner should understand. If you’ve waited until the last minute and you’re in a crisis – outsource the work and learn from your mistakes. If it’s all just been a bungle, make sure you both move on from the project on good terms!

8. You just didn’t cut the mustard: Maybe you wanted to try something new, or you jumped in to an opportunity too quickly! Your services are usually excellent, but for some reason you just couldn’t find your feet on this one. It happens to the best of us at times, but…..do you end up with an unhappy collaboration? How can you salvage the situation? It’s best to come clean and be honest that you just didn’t cut the mustard. Offer a discount on the project and see if you can resolve any issues in order to finish on good terms. In a co-working space recommendations are key to landing a project; prove you can do, by providing free work in the future to compensate for a not so great job.
